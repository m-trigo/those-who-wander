Those Who Wander

Player Character Overview

Ability Scores

Each character has a set of five numeric values, called ability scores, that range from -4 to +4 and represent their physical attributes:

Strength, which represents characters’ physical power

Endurance, which represents characters’ fortitude and resilience

Perception, which represents characters’ awareness

Agility, which represents characters’ nimbleness and reflexes

Dexterity, which represents character’s coordination

Inventory

In addition to its physical capabilities, characters must rely in a variety of tools, weapons and gear carried with them. Every item available to a character is listed under the character’s inventory.

Health

Health is a measure of the character’s vitality and distance from death. A player character’s health is their endurance score +10.  Characters die when their health reaches zero.

Wounds

Any physical damage characters have suffered that has not yet fully healed is represented in a list of wounds. Wounds have a fictional description, indicating their location, and reduce the character’s health by the amount of damage that caused the wound.

Stamina

Stamina is the extra reserve of inner strength that characters still have since their last rest period. Player characters can spend their stamina to apply effort to an action and increase their chance of success. The maximum amount of stamina a character can have is the same as their maximum health.

How to Play

Once the game starts, the GM will start narrating the fiction. From this point on, what you say describes what your character does, attempts to do or intends to do, but it’s the GM who arbitrates and describes what happens in the fiction.

Only in cases when the GM’s arbitration is not enough to decide the outcome of an action, the following rules are used to assist it.

Ability Checks

When ability checks are called for, the GM first decides which ability score is the most relevant to the action. The player then rolls a twenty-sided die (d20) and adds the character’s named ability score to the result. If the total exceeds 10, the action succeeds and the player either accomplishes their desired outcome or makes progress toward that outcome. Otherwise, the action either fails or succeeds with a compromise. In either case, the total is not indicative of the action’s quality: an action is equally successful whether the total was 11 or 24.

Advantage & Disadvantage

Ability checks for certain actions may be granted an exceptional chance of success (or failure) and are said to have advantage (or disadvantage). When that happens, the player rolls a second d20 when making the ability check. If the action has advantage, the higher of the two rolls is used. If the action has disadvantage, the lower of the two rolls is used instead.

If circumstances cause the action to have both advantage and disadvantage, the action is considered to have neither and ability check is resolved as normal.

Critical Success & Critical Failure

An action is a critical success when it has advantage and both of its dice rolls make it succeed. Conversely, if the action had disadvantage and both rolls make it fail, the action critically fails.

Inventory

Every item in a character’s inventory must be listed as being carried in one of four ways:

Hand, for items being carried in the character’s hands

Belt, for items strapped to the character’s belt

Backpack, for items being carried inside a backpack the character is carrying

Strap, for items strapped to a backpack the character is carrying

Worn, for items being worn around the character’s body

Characters can only carry a single item in each of their hand, belt and strap locations. The backpack location is represented as a 3x2 square grid. The number of squares of the backpack an item occupies is determined by its weight and size.

Effects of Strength

If your strength is positive, you may consider the weight property of some your items as being of one type lighter. If your strength is negative, you must consider the weight property of some of your items as being of one type heavier. The number of items affected in either case equals the number in your strength score (so both -2 and +2 effect 2 items).

Wounds

Healing

If proper treatment is administered, a characters’ wound starts to heal and reduces 1 less health per day of uninterrupted rest. A wound that reduces no health is said to be healed and is removed from the character’s wounds list.

Conflicts

Turn Order

Once the chaos of a time-sensitive situation sets in, or when time and distance are important, the resolution of actions takes a more structured order: actions from opposing sides of the conflict happen in turns.

Initiative

At the start of a conflict, player characters have a chance to acquire initiative and act once before the opposing forces first round of actions. The turn order is always: players characters with initiative, other sides, all players characters, other sides, players characters, other sides, and so on.

Initiative might be acquired through the fiction or through an ability check. The fiction might grant initiative to a character or prevent a character from acquiring it. Otherwise, players can acquire it by succeeding a perception or agility ability check.

Actions

Once it’s their side’s turn to act, a character engaged in the conflict gets to do something that takes up to 5 seconds inside the game fiction.

Moving

On their turn, every character can move somewhere nearby before his action or forgo the action and move somewhere far away instead. Distances in combat are rough estimates based on the following table:

Weapons

Weapons are tools specialized in causing wounds. They have an additional property that describes their damage potential and a set of special tags that describe other qualities.

Damage Die

The damage die of a weapon is the die to be rolled to determine the damage dealt to a target after a successful attack is performed with that weapon.

If the attack is a critical success or the attack was successful without an ability check, then the weapon deals the maximum possible damage of its damage die.

Tags

Damage isn’t everything for a weapon. Like a good tool, every weapon works best when applied to specific situations. Weapon tags provide either a downside to the weapon or a useful feature.

Hidden Results

In some situations, the result of an attempted action is not immediately evident to the characters in the fiction. For example, when sneaking around or trying to deceiving someone. In those scenarios, the GM will make a secret roll which might change the result of the ability check. Players know about the secret roll, but not its result.

 

The Secret Roll

Before the player’s ability check, the GM rolls a d6 and keeps its result hidden. If the hidden result is 4, 5 or 6 the ability check is unaffected. Otherwise, the value of the player’s dice roll is “reversed”, meaning that  lower numbers were actually better than higher ones.

For example, if the dice roll is reversed and the player rolled a 17, then the result was actually a 4. Whereas if he had rolled a 2, the result would be a 19.

Ability scores are not affected by the secret roll and added to the dice roll result after the dice roll result was affected (or not) by the secret roll.

Group Checks

When characters are trying to accomplish something as a group or working together to accomplish the same task, the Referee first determines whether the group will succeed if any member succeeds or if the group will fail if any member fails. In the first scenario, the character with the highest modifiers will perform the ability check, in the second scenario, the character with the lowest modifiers does it. The Referee might then apply bonuses for teamwork.

Codify into a rule the “Murky Mirror” principle: Immersion

Social interaction and other things that the player can do, like saying how to disarm a trap, have no character sheet mechanic.

Similarly, what the players do in real life directly relates what the characters are doing.
